Vending
Phone Numbers

How a phone number gets from Twilio's wholesale inventory onto a customer's account — and back again when they leave. This is the mechanics of the money leg.

Money leg Voice only · no A2P needed Blocked on: Mac-side drain
The whole idea in one line
Twilio is the wholesaler. We are the retailer. The customer never learns Twilio exists.

They pay us one bill. We pay Twilio. The number is ours, issued to them — which is the only reason any of this is defensible: a fork can copy every line of our MIT-licensed code and still not have a phone number to give anyone.

The trap we are not building: "customer signs up for their own Twilio, buys their own number, then also pays us." That makes them pay twice, gives us zero marginal cost, and therefore leaves us nothing to enforce. It is the same dead end that disqualified the on-duty loop as a paid feature.

Diagram 01

The wire — what happens when the phone rings

The Mac is never in the synchronous path. Twilio needs an answer in milliseconds; a laptop might be shut. So the relay answers, and the Mac catches up later.

This is the only thing standing between us and revenue

Every decision on this page — pricing, provisioning, compliance, churn — is downstream of a websocket client that does not exist. There is no Realtime dependency in mix.exs, no drain process in the supervision tree, and no Twilio REST client anywhere in lib/. Build the drain before building anything else here.

One bill in, two bills out. The margin lives in the gap, and it is only healthy because the wholesale cost of a number is small — which is exactly why usage, not the number, is the thing that can hurt us.

The hard parts

Usage caps are a pricing requirement, not a feature

A flat subscription against metered voice minutes and per-minute transcription means one chatty or abused account is an unbounded bill we volunteered for. The number is $1–2. The minutes are the risk. Cap voice minutes per account per month, degrade honestly when the cap is hit ("mailbox full"), and surface the meter in the app. This must ship with the first paid number, not after the first surprise invoice.

An agent with a phone can be socially engineered

It is our Twilio account and our carrier reputation on the line, not the customer's. A prompt-injected agent that starts dialing, or a greeting that gets weaponised, lands on us — and carrier trust, once lost, is not something you buy back. Per-account subaccounts contain the blast radius; a Sentinel-visible kill switch stops it. Both are prerequisites, not polish.

Releasing a number is a one-way door with a person on the other side

Churn is not a database row here. A released number goes back into the pool and is reissued to a stranger — so someone who cancels, changes their mind, and comes back has permanently lost the number they gave to people. That is a real harm and it will generate real anger. It is why parked exists. Park it, tell them exactly when it dies, and let them buy it back until then.

The path

Order of operations

Phase 0 — Make one number work, for one person: you

blocking · no money required beyond the number
  • Upgrade the Twilio trial to a paid account; buy one real number.
  • Build the Mac-side drain — a Supabase Realtime websocket client in the supervision tree that pulls telephony_events down into SQLite. Everything else on this page is blocked on this.
  • Dogfood it. Give the number to friends. Let it take real messages for a fortnight.

Phase 1 — Vend by hand (concierge)

first ~10 customers · zero automation
  • They pay. You buy the number, you point the webhook, you type it into their account.
  • It does not scale, and that is the point: do not build a vending machine before you know anyone wants a number. Ten manual provisions cost you an evening and teach you what the automation must actually do.
  • What you learn here — how they churn, what they call it, what they ask for — is worth more than the code you would have written.

Phase 2 — The vending machine

only once Phase 1 hurts
  • Twilio REST client. Subaccount per customer. Search → buy → configure webhook, automatically.
  • MoR webhook → entitlement → provision. The state machine above, in code.
  • Usage caps and the kill switch ship here or earlier. Not later.

Phase 3 — Lifecycle & dunning

the unglamorous half
  • Payment fails → suspend (not release). Card fixed → resume, same number.
  • Cancels → park, with a countdown the customer can see and reverse.
  • Grace ends → release, and only then.

Phase 4 — SMS

gated on the LLC
  • Form the entity, get the EIN, register the A2P brand and campaign, survive carrier review.
  • Then, and only then, turn on the SMS threads already sitting in the /phone UI.
Unanswered

Open questions

Local number, or toll-free?
Local reads personal, which suits "your agent's phone." Toll-free carries a lighter verification path for messaging later. This choice is easy to make now and painful to change once numbers are issued.
How long is parked, and do we charge for it?
A "keep my number" fee at a couple of dollars a month would exactly cover the rent, turn a pure loss into a break-even, and be a genuine kindness to someone between jobs. It is also another SKU to defend.
Can a customer take their number with them?
Porting out is the honest answer to "what if I leave," and it is a trust signal worth more than the churn it enables. Not v1 — but say so out loud rather than staying quiet about it.
What is the voice-minute cap, in numbers?
Unanswerable until Phase 0 has run for a fortnight and we know what a normal month of messages actually looks like. That is one of the reasons Phase 0 exists.
